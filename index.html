The Friend Who's
Always Available

College students turn to generative AI models such as Claude and ChatGPT in lieu of traditional therapy and friends

College students are using AI for more than schoolwork

Dating in New York City can be stressful. For some, the fast paced nature of the city can call for additional emotional support and validation. Cecilia, an international master's student from China at Columbia, talked to a familiar voice. But instead of sitting on a cushioned couch and relaying her emotions to a living "shrink" or therapist, Cecilia poured her qualms into the generative artificial intelligence model ChatGPT.

"In New York I'm not familiar with the dating culture and I got hurt several times," Cecilia said. "I'm also getting used to it by using ChatGPT ." She adds it's not ChatGPT alone that helps, but moreover the ability to vent to someone who is not emotional. "I need a person to be there to hear all my stuff. That's it."

College students are pouring their issues into generative AI models such as Claude and ChatGPT to alleviate stress, and in some cases, use AI as a replacement or as an alternative to therapy completely. Young adults faced with intense challenges, transitions and important life decisions see the automated models as an antidote to anxiety or discomfort with friends.

This is a nationwide trend. Around 1 in 8 adolescents aged 18–21 are using AI tools, according to The School of Public Health at Brown University, RAND and Harvard researchers.

University of Toronto student, Nawal Shebe, a first-year and prospective Geography and Environmental Management major, frequently uses AI to complete her assignments. According to her, the pressure of academia has forced her to rely on Claude and ChatGPT. "In the beginning, I actually tried my best not to [use AI]," she said. "I've used it more so in the second semester."

Shebe started relying on AI around 10th grade, first asking it questions for school assignments and academic work. When she was around a senior in high school, she started turning to the model in moments of social isolation. Now, as a first year student in college, faced with intense academic pressure, she's asking ChatGPT about her future if she were to drop out. "How would I even go about that, so I don't end up being like, unsuccessful or like homeless or something [in] 10 years," she said. "I want to ask that [things] are on a larger scale."

Like Shebe, Cecilia originally used the AI models as an academic tool. Cecilia relies on three AI models heavily in order to conduct macro research for her masters program: Clause, ChatGPT and DeepSeek. But she turns to ChatGPT the most for emotional support.

"The reason I use ChatGPT is because I've been using it for two years and I'm familiar with his voice," she said. "He will always say, 'You're doing it well, you're doing it right and so don't do that. [Don't] punish yourself.'"

We designed a survey and invited students to share about their AI usage outside of academic or professional contexts and received 15 responses. According to our results, 71.4% of respondents indicated that they seek AI for help with decision-making, 42.9% indicated that they ask AI for advice on social and personal problems, and 28.6% indicated that they use AI as a venting outlet or listening ear. Of course, as a small scale sample this cannot be used to represent the greater campus community, but simply represents the situations of a small group of students.

Why are students turning to AI over human friendships?

Both Shebe and Cecilia are faced with intense life changes, using the services to vent. But instead of relying on a social network, they had reasons to turn to services as a way to cope with these transitions.

"It's like different kinds of venting," Shebe said. The first year found that in moments of career anxiety where she's searching for pragmatic advice about the future, she turns to AI. But in moments of emotional distress, she turns to her friends for help. "When I want specific answers to something that's stressing me out, or I want to vent about something, I would go to AI. But if it's something where I'm looking more for just understanding and comfort rather than answers or solutions, I'd probably go to a friend." In addition to the "different kinds of venting," Shebe also added the competitiveness and isolation of her respective university pushed her to use AI .

The busy schedules of college students can further exasperate the desire for an immediate fix. "And also the reason I talk to it a lot is because you can talk to your friend, right? But your friend is not always available. They have other plans, schedules, it can not always be there for you, but ChatGPT can," Cecilia said. She added that she appreciates that AI models are not emotional the way a friend can be.

In our survey respondents answered why they would choose AI rather than talking to a human being, whether a friend or a therapist. 35.7% of respondents cited the advantage of AI being judgment-free: telling AI your problems won't change its opinion of you, nor will it lead to any social consequences. People feel less embarrassed and more comfortable when they talk to AI. 28.7% of responses emphasize the readiness of AI. Other highlights of AI also include that it won't be emotionally impacted when it's vented to, and it is less subject to bias.

But with the possibility of support there are risks. Nathan Gorelick, an assistant professor at Barnard College and author of the book The Unwritten Enlightenment: Literature between Ideology and the Unconscious shared his thoughts with us on why some may be skeptical about the use of AI.

"I've taken a pretty strong stance against using it," Gorelick said. In the context of a classroom he argues that it takes away from the learning process in his classes when he finds AI generated essays.

But when asked about AI being used for student's wellbeing Gorelick paused thoughtfully. "I wouldn't have moral judgment on students," Gorelick began. "I am wary of the capitalization of wellness, the way in which mental health is a kind of eminently exploitable domain when it comes to AI developers and the broader attention economy, I think the human mind is the social mind. And again, while I wouldn't want to pass summary judgment, I do think there are dangers to the automation or organization of mental health that in the by and large we as a community or society"

For young people, doubts do appear in the use of AI, such as data phishing. "I mean, there's a lot of times where I'm like, Oh, maybe I shouldn't put this information about myself, or maybe I shouldn't be so specific about this because, you know, I don't know the capabilities of it," Shebe said.

Despite these risks, generative AI seems to be an imminent fix for an individual. "Sometimes I feel like I just need to make my thoughts clear," Cecilia said. "I already have the answer in my heart, in my mind, I just need to make it more clear and talking with ChatGPT can help me with that. And then I just shut up and I just turn it off."

Closing reflection

Something is lost.
Something is found.

As generative AI becomes more embedded in daily life, the line between tool and confidant continues to blur. For students navigating the pressures of academia, social complexity, and early adulthood, AI offers something rare: a listener with infinite patience, no agenda, and no judgment. Whether that represents a substitute for human connection — or simply a different kind of it — remains an open question.
